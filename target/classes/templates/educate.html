Importance of Trees

                                                

Dying Species

Bayonet Spider Orchid


Bayonet Spider Orchid is a small orchid up to 10 cm high.  Flowering occurs during September. 
The orchid develops a single leaf and a single green, brown and maroon flower, with bayonet shaped clubs on petals and sepals.  The flower has a strong musky smell which attracts a certain species of wasp.  This wasp plays an important role in the orchid's pollination process.
Habitat loss, grazing by rabbits and competition with native and introduced plants are the main causes of the Bayonet Spider Orchid's decline.

Buxton Gum


Buxton Gum is a small tree up to 8 m high with low glaucous foliage.  The small, hart shaped leaves have crenulate margins and embrace the stem.  The species produces cream, strongly scented flowers in spring which attract nectar feeders such as honeyeaters and insects.
Habitat loss is a major threat to Buxton Gum.  Changes to water regimes and fire patterns, grazing and trampling by livestock and wild deer, and competition with other plant species, particularly the parasitic Dodder Laurel Cassytha melantha, also contribute to its decline.

Canberra Daisy

It is a perennial herb and member of the daisy family.
It is threatened by disturbance and changes to its habitat. 
Fire management and weed control help to protect the habitat of Canberra Daisy.

Epacris Stuartii

Epacris stuartii is a small shrub with a few, slender, upright branches.  The small, flat leaves are attached to stalks.  Single white flowers at the top part of the branch appear during October and November.
Epacris stuartii is threatened by the introduction of the soil fungus Phytophthora cinnamomi.  The fungus is easily spread by people who visit the nearby historic site and unknowingly carry infected soil on their shoes.  Intense or frequent fires, and fires followed by dry weather, threaten the survival of seedlings.  Natural threats such as storms may also cause serious damage to plants.

Hairy Quandong

Hairy Quandong is a slender rainforest tree up to 15 metres high.  It sometimes has several stems and the bark is fawn or cream.  Flowers are a pale green and form pendulous clusters.  Hairy Quandong flowers in November and December.
The main threat to Hairy Quandong is the invasion of weeds such as Lantana.  Fire is also a potential threat especially if it is severe.

Pink Pimelia

Pink Pimelea is a spreading plant which grows to about 50 cm high.  It flowers from September to May.  White flowers tinged with pink form clusters on the top of each stem.  As the plant matures, only the leaves and flowers at the tip of the stem remain.
Threats to Pink Pimelea include habitat loss, soil erosion, rubbish dumping and uncontrolled access.  Weeds and fire need careful management to reduce
their effects on Pink Pimelea.

Rose Mallee


Rose Mallee is a low spreading tree up to 4 metres high.  It has silvery grey leaves which are attached directly to the stem.  Flowering is between March and November.  The red flowers are 7.5 cm across, with yellow anthers which contain lots of nectar.
The main threats to Rose Mallee in disturbed sites include grazing by sheep, roadworks, damage by vehicles, fire and weeds.  Rabbits also degrade areas where Rose Mallee grows.

Scaly Myrtle


Scaly Myrtle is a medium sized bushy tree up to 18 m tall.  It has smooth, glossy leaves which have a myrtle smell when crushed.  Leaves are up to 6 cm long and arranged in opposite pairs on the branchlets.  Flowers are white with four or five small petals and appear from November to December.  The fruit is black, round and soft.  The bark of the tree is grey/brown and flaky.
Much of the dry rainforest where Angle-stemmed Myrtle grows has been cleared for urban and rural development.  Trampling of plants by cattle and people, rubbish dumping, and competition from weeds and native vines prevent new plants from growing.

Tunbridge Buttercup


Tunbridge Buttercup is a small herb with yellow flowers.  Flowering occurs from October until
March.
Grazing, soil compaction and disturbance caused by sheep and cattle affect the ecology of the Tunbridge Buttercup's habitat.  Changes to water quantity and quality in the lagoons where this species occurs also threaten its survival.

Wongan Hills Trigger Plant


Wongan Hills Trigger Plant is a small herb with grey green leaves clustered at the base.  Flowers are creamy pink to white and are attached to stalks which grow from the centre of the base.  The Wongan Hills Trigger Plant flowers in September.
Sheep grazing, removal of gravel, habitat loss and disturbance, tracks and roadworks are threats to Wongan Hills Trigger Plant.

Volunteering Services


Volunteering for plants would help you to develop your minds and become more empathetic towards them. It will help you to connect with many people around you who are enthusiastic just like you. Government of Victoria is also promoting volunteering which will help you to nourish a tree along with your pals.
